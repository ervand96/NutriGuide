# The MIND Diet: A Practical Guide to Support Brain Health

## What Is the MIND Diet and How It Supports Brain Health

The MIND diet — short for Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay — is an eating pattern designed to support long-term brain health. Researchers at Rush University Medical Center developed it by combining the Mediterranean diet and the DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) diet, then emphasizing foods most strongly linked to cognitive outcomes in observational studies.

Unlike a rigid meal plan, the MIND diet is a set of food-group targets you aim for each week. The idea is not perfection but consistent patterns: eating more of the foods associated with better brain aging and less of those tied to higher risk of cognitive decline.

Observational research has found that people who follow the MIND diet more closely may have a lower risk of Alzheimer’s disease and slower cognitive decline with age. These studies do not prove cause and effect, but they align with broader evidence that diets rich in vegetables, berries, whole grains, and unsaturated fats support cardiovascular and metabolic health — both of which influence the brain.

**Practical tip:** Treat the MIND diet as a scorecard, not an all-or-nothing rulebook. Even partial adherence has been associated with benefits in some studies, so small, steady changes matter more than overhauling your entire kitchen overnight.

## Foods to Eat on the MIND Diet

The MIND diet highlights specific foods and frequencies:

- **Green leafy vegetables** — at least six servings per week (spinach, kale, collards, romaine, mixed salad greens)
- **Other vegetables** — at least one serving daily
- **Berries** — at least two servings per week (blueberries and strawberries are most studied)
- **Nuts** — at least five servings per week (a small handful counts as one serving)
- **Whole grains** — at least three servings daily (oats, brown rice, quinoa, whole-wheat bread)
- **Beans and legumes** — at least four servings per week
- **Poultry** — at least two servings per week
- **Fish** — at least one serving per week, especially fatty fish rich in omega-3 fats
- **Olive oil** — as your primary cooking and dressing oil
- **Wine** — optional, up to one glass daily if you already drink alcohol (not recommended if you do not)

These foods provide fiber, antioxidants, polyphenols, and healthy fats that support blood vessel function and may reduce oxidative stress in the brain.

**Practical tips:**

- Batch-cook a large salad base on Sunday and add greens to eggs, soups, or grain bowls through the week.
- Keep frozen berries on hand for smoothies, yogurt, or oatmeal — they count and are often more affordable than fresh.
- Swap refined grains gradually: start with one whole-grain meal per day before building from there.

## Foods to Limit or Avoid

The MIND diet also sets upper limits on foods linked to poorer cognitive outcomes in population studies:

- **Red and processed meats** — fewer than four servings of red meat per week; minimize processed meats like bacon, sausage, and deli meats
- **Butter and stick margarine** — less than one tablespoon daily
- **Cheese** — less than one serving per week
- **Pastries and sweets** — fewer than five servings per week
- **Fried or fast food** — less than one serving per week

These foods tend to be high in saturated fat, refined carbohydrates, sodium, or trans fats. Eating them occasionally is normal; the goal is to keep them from dominating your weekly pattern.

**Practical tips:**

- When dining out, choose grilled fish or poultry with vegetables instead of fried options — one of the simplest ways to stay within MIND limits.
- Use olive oil, avocado, or nut butters in place of butter on toast and in cooking where flavor allows.
- Save pastries and desserts for intentional treats rather than daily habits; pairing a smaller portion with berries can satisfy a sweet craving while staying closer to the plan.

The MIND diet works best as a long-term pattern paired with other brain-supporting habits: regular physical activity, adequate sleep, social connection, and management of blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol. Talk to your doctor or a registered dietitian before making major dietary changes, especially if you take medications or have existing health conditions.

## Sample MIND Diet Meal Plan for One Week

The MIND diet emphasizes leafy greens, berries, nuts, whole grains, olive oil, fish, and poultry while limiting red meat, butter, cheese, fried foods, and sweets. This sample week shows how those foods can fit into everyday meals.

**Monday**
- Breakfast: Oatmeal with blueberries, walnuts, and cinnamon
- Lunch: Spinach salad with chickpeas, cherry tomatoes, cucumber, and olive oil dressing
- Dinner: Baked salmon with quinoa and roasted broccoli

**Tuesday**
- Breakfast: Greek yogurt with strawberries and sliced almonds
- Lunch: Whole-grain wrap with grilled chicken, mixed greens, and avocado
- Dinner: Lentil soup with a side of kale sautéed in olive oil

**Wednesday**
- Breakfast: Scrambled eggs with sautéed spinach and whole-grain toast
- Lunch: Tuna salad (olive oil–based) over mixed greens
- Dinner: Turkey chili with beans and a side salad

**Thursday**
- Breakfast: Smoothie with spinach, banana, frozen berries, and ground flaxseed
- Lunch: Brown rice bowl with roasted vegetables and hummus
- Dinner: Baked cod with sweet potato and green beans

**Friday**
- Breakfast: Whole-grain cereal with milk and raspberries
- Lunch: Leftover turkey chili
- Dinner: Grilled chicken with farro and roasted Brussels sprouts

**Saturday**
- Breakfast: Whole-grain toast with almond butter and sliced banana
- Lunch: Mixed green salad with walnuts, apple, and grilled salmon
- Dinner: Vegetable stir-fry with tofu or chicken over brown rice

**Sunday**
- Breakfast: Vegetable omelet with mushrooms and peppers
- Lunch: Bean and vegetable soup with whole-grain bread
- Dinner: Baked trout with roasted asparagus and quinoa

## How to Start the MIND Diet and Stay Consistent

Starting the MIND diet works best with small, repeatable changes rather than a full pantry overhaul. Pick two or three targets for the first week: add one leafy green serving daily, swap refined grains for whole grains at one meal, and include berries twice weekly. These steps align with the diet’s strongest evidence-based recommendations without requiring strict calorie counting.

**Build a simple shopping list.** Stock olive oil, leafy greens, frozen berries, nuts, whole grains (oats, brown rice, quinoa), canned beans, fish, and poultry. Limit red meat, butter, full-fat cheese, pastries, and fried snacks. MyProtein offers plain whey or plant-based protein powder if you want a simple addition to smoothies, but whole foods should remain the base of your meals.

**Prep ahead.** Wash greens, cook a batch of grains, and portion nuts into small containers. Pre-cut vegetables reduce friction on busy days. Frozen fish fillets and canned beans make it easier to hit weekly fish and legume targets.

**Track progress loosely.** A simple checklist—greens most days, berries twice weekly, nuts most days, fish once weekly—helps you stay on track without obsessing over perfection. Missing a target one day does not undo prior progress.

**Handle eating out.** Choose grilled fish or poultry, ask for extra vegetables, and request dressings on the side. Skip fried sides and heavy cheese-based dishes when possible.

**Stay consistent long term.** Link new habits to existing routines: add berries to breakfast, use olive oil at dinner, or keep nuts at your desk for a planned snack. Consistency over months matters more than perfect adherence in a single week.

The MIND diet offers a practical, food-first approach to brain health by emphasizing vegetables, berries, nuts, whole grains, and fish while limiting foods linked to poorer cognitive outcomes. A one-week meal plan and gradual habit changes make it manageable for daily life. Talk with your doctor or a registered dietitian if you have medical conditions, take blood thinners, or need personalized guidance before making major dietary changes.
