## What to Look for in a Creatine Supplement

**Form and purity.** Creatine monohydrate remains the default choice because decades of research support its safety and effectiveness. Look for products labeled as Creapure or third-party tested, which helps confirm purity and reduce contamination risk.

**Dosing clarity.** A standard maintenance dose is 3–5 grams per day. Some powders include extra ingredients you may not need. If you want simplicity, a single-ingredient product with a clear scoop size is easier to use consistently.

**Solubility and mixability.** Creatine can feel gritty or settle at the bottom of a shaker. Micronized powders often dissolve better in water or a protein shake. If texture bothers you, that matters more than fancy branding.

**Value per gram.** Compare cost by grams of creatine per serving, not just tub size. Larger bags from trusted retailers often cost less per dose than small pre-workout blends with a tiny creatine amount.

## Creatine Monohydrate vs Other Forms: Which Is Best?

**Creatine monohydrate** is the form used in most clinical trials. It is well absorbed, widely available, and usually the cheapest option. Claims that newer forms work dramatically better are often overstated.

**Creatine HCl** dissolves more easily and may cause less bloating for some people, but it typically costs more per effective dose. Research support is thinner than for monohydrate.

**Buffered creatine** and **creatine ethyl ester** were marketed as superior alternatives, yet studies have not shown clear advantages over standard monohydrate for strength or muscle gains.

**Liquid creatine** and pre-mixed drinks can degrade over time, which may reduce potency before you finish the bottle.

For most gym-goers, athletes, and casual lifters, monohydrate is the best starting point. Other forms may suit people with digestive sensitivity or strong preferences about taste and texture, but they are optional upgrades, not requirements.

**Practical tip:** Loading is optional. A daily 3–5 g dose builds muscle creatine stores over a few weeks without the higher initial doses some protocols suggest. Drink enough water, since creatine pulls fluid into muscle tissue. That is normal and not a sign of poor product quality.

## How to Take Creatine Safely and Effectively

Creatine monohydrate remains the most studied form and the standard most people should start with. A typical maintenance dose is **3–5 grams per day**, taken consistently rather than only on training days. Timing matters less than daily use; many people take it post-workout with a meal or shake, but morning or evening works equally well if you stay consistent.

A **loading phase** (roughly 20 g per day split into 4 doses for 5–7 days) can saturate muscle stores faster, but it is optional. Skipping loading and taking 3–5 g daily still reaches full saturation within about three to four weeks, often with less stomach discomfort.

Mix creatine into water, juice, or a protein shake. **MyProtein Creatine Monohydrate** and **Optimum Nutrition Micronized Creatine Monohydrate** (both available on their respective sites) dissolve reasonably well and are straightforward daily options. If monohydrate causes bloating or digestive upset, **Kaged Creatine HCl** on iHerb is a smaller-dose alternative that some people tolerate better.

Stay well hydrated. Creatine draws water into muscle tissue, which supports performance but can increase fluid needs. Drink water regularly throughout the day, especially during intense training blocks.

Creatine is generally safe for healthy adults at recommended doses. People with kidney disease, those on medications that affect kidney function, or anyone with a pre-existing medical condition should consult a doctor before starting. Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid supplementation unless a clinician advises otherwise.

Store powder in a cool, dry place and use a dry scoop to prevent clumping. Micronized formulas from brands like **California Gold Nutrition Creatine Monohydrate** (iHerb) tend to mix more easily than non-micronized versions.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Creatine Supplements

**Does creatine cause weight gain?**  
Yes, but mostly from increased water content in muscle, not fat. You may see a 1–3 lb increase on the scale within the first week. Over time, creatine supports strength and training volume, which can contribute to lean mass gains when paired with resistance training and adequate protein.

**Is creatine only for bodybuilders?**  
No. Research supports benefits for strength athletes, sprinters, team-sport players, and older adults concerned about muscle loss. Endurance athletes may see smaller direct performance gains, though some still use it for recovery support.

**Do I need to cycle creatine?**  
There is no strong evidence that cycling on and off improves results or prevents tolerance. Continuous daily use at 3–5 g is the approach supported by most research.

**Will creatine harm my kidneys?**  
In healthy individuals, creatine at standard doses has not been shown to damage kidneys. Blood creatinine levels may rise slightly because creatine breaks down into creatinine — this is expected and not the same as kidney damage. Anyone with existing kidney issues should speak with a healthcare provider first.

**Creatine monohydrate vs. other forms — which is best?**  
Monohydrate has the largest body of evidence and is usually the most cost-effective choice. Newer forms like HCl, buffered creatine, or creatine ethyl ester are marketed for better absorption, but monohydrate consistently performs well in studies. **MyProtein Creapure Creatine Monohydrate** is a widely used pharmaceutical-grade option if purity is a priority.

**Can women take creatine?**  
Yes. Women can use the same 3–5 g daily dose. Benefits include improved strength, power, and lean mass when combined with resistance training, without causing excessive bulk.

**Does creatine cause hair loss?**  
One small study suggested a possible link to increased DHT, but larger reviews have not confirmed creatine causes hair loss. The evidence remains inconclusive.

**Should I take creatine with carbs or protein?**  
Insulin may slightly enhance uptake, so taking creatine with a meal or post-workout shake that includes carbohydrates or protein is reasonable, though not required for it to work.
