## What Are Electrolytes and When Do You Need a Supplement?

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electrical charge in body fluids. The main ones in supplements are sodium, potassium, magnesium, and calcium. They help regulate fluid balance, nerve signals, and muscle contractions.

You usually get enough from a balanced diet. Whole foods like bananas, leafy greens, dairy, and lightly salted meals cover most needs for sedentary adults.

A supplement can make sense when losses outpace intake. Common situations include:

- **Heavy or long sweating** — endurance training, hot-weather workouts, or outdoor labor
- **Illness** — vomiting, diarrhea, or fever that depletes fluids and minerals
- **Low-carb or ketogenic eating** — lower insulin can increase sodium excretion early on
- **Restrictive diets** — very low sodium, chronic undereating, or limited variety

Signs you may need more electrolytes include persistent muscle cramps, unusual fatigue during exercise, dizziness when standing, or headaches after hard sessions in heat. Mild thirst after a normal workout often resolves with water and regular meals alone.

**Practical tip:** Match the product to the situation. A light daily powder suits general hydration support. Higher-sodium formulas are better for heavy sweaters, while lower-sodium options may fit people monitoring blood pressure.

## Key Electrolytes, Dosages, and How to Choose the Right Formula

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electrical charge and support fluid balance, nerve signaling, and muscle contraction. The four that matter most in supplements are sodium, potassium, magnesium, and chloride.

**Sodium** is lost in the highest amounts through sweat. General guidance for active adults in hot conditions or during prolonged exercise often falls in the range of 300–700 mg per hour of heavy sweating, though individual needs vary widely. People on low-carb diets may benefit from higher daily sodium intake from food and supplements combined — many clinicians suggest roughly 3,000–5,000 mg total sodium per day for keto-adapted adults, but this should be adjusted based on blood pressure, kidney function, and medical advice.

**Potassium** supports muscle function and blood pressure regulation. Supplement forms typically provide 100–200 mg per serving; most daily potassium should still come from food sources like leafy greens, avocado, and salmon. Avoid megadosing potassium supplements without medical supervision, especially if you take blood pressure or kidney medications.

**Magnesium** aids muscle relaxation and energy metabolism. Glycinate and citrate forms are common in electrolyte blends. A typical supplemental dose ranges from 100–400 mg of elemental magnesium daily, depending on diet and tolerance. Loose stools can occur at higher doses.

**Chloride** usually accompanies sodium and helps maintain fluid balance. It is rarely dosed separately but appears in most complete electrolyte formulas.

When choosing a formula, check four things on the label: total sodium per serving, added sugar or carbohydrate content, whether magnesium is included, and serving size realism. Products that list only trace amounts of sodium while emphasizing vitamins are often poor choices for sweat replacement. Third-party testing is a plus but not universal in this category.
