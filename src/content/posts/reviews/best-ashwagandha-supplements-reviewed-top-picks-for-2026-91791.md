# Best Ashwagandha Supplements Reviewed: Top Picks for 2026

Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*) is one of the most studied adaptogenic herbs in modern supplement research. As interest in stress support, sleep quality, and recovery continues to grow, choosing a reliable product matters more than chasing the highest milligram count on the label.

## What Is Ashwagandha and What Does the Research Say?

Ashwagandha is a root extract used for centuries in Ayurvedic medicine. Today it is most often taken for stress management, sleep, cognitive function, and exercise recovery. The active compounds commonly discussed in research are withanolides, though whole-root extracts are also widely used.

Evidence is promising but not definitive. Several randomized trials suggest ashwagandha may help reduce perceived stress and cortisol levels in adults under chronic stress. Some studies also report modest improvements in sleep quality, anxiety scores, and subjective well-being. For physical performance, research has shown small gains in strength, power output, and recovery markers in trained and untrained adults, though results vary by dose, extract type, and study design.

Important limitations apply. Many studies use proprietary extracts such as KSM-66 or Sensoril, which are not identical to generic root powders. Doses in research often range from 300–600 mg daily of standardized extract, taken consistently for 8–12 weeks. Ashwagandha is generally well tolerated, but it may cause drowsiness, digestive upset, or headache in some people. It is not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding, and people with thyroid conditions, autoimmune disorders, or those taking sedatives or thyroid medication should speak with a healthcare provider before use.

**Practical tips:**
- Look for products that state the extract type and withanolide content on the label.
- Give it time: most clinical trials run at least two months before measuring outcomes.
- Take it with food if you experience stomach discomfort.
- If you notice excessive sedation, reduce the dose or take it in the evening.

## Ashwagandha Forms, Dosage, and What to Look for on the Label

Ashwagandha supplements are not interchangeable. Most quality products use *Withania somnifera* root extract rather than leaf, because clinical research on stress, sleep, and exercise recovery has focused on root-based formulas.

**Common forms you will see on iHerb and MyProtein:**

- **KSM-66** — A full-spectrum root extract standardized to at least 5% withanolides. It appears in many capsule formulas, including **MyProtein Ashwagandha KSM-66 Capsules** and brands such as **NOW Foods KSM-66 Ashwagandha** on iHerb.
- **Sensoril** — A root-and-leaf extract standardized for withanolide glycosides and oligosaccharides. **Paradise Herbs, Sensoril Ashwagandha** is one example available on iHerb.
- **Whole root powder** — Less concentrated and often less standardized. Useful for traditional use, but harder to match research doses unless the label states extract equivalency.
- **Capsules vs tablets** — Capsules dominate; tablets can work but check whether the active ingredient is listed as extract or raw powder.

**Typical research-based dosing** for standardized root extracts is **300–600 mg per day**, often split into two doses with meals. Some studies use up to 600 mg twice daily for short periods, but starting at the lower end is sensible if you are new to adaptogens. If the label lists raw powder without extract strength, compare carefully — you may need several grams of powder to approximate a 300 mg extract dose.

**What to look for on the label:**

- **Standardization** — Withanolide percentage (often 5% for KSM-66) or a clear Sensoril trademark
- **Part of plant** — Root extract is preferred over leaf-only products for most goals
- **Amount per serving** — Match extract milligrams, not just capsule count
- **Fillers and allergens** — Check for unnecessary blends that hide the active dose
- **Third-party testing** — USP, NSF, or brand-published COAs add confidence, especially for long-term use

**Practical tip:** Take ashwagandha with food if you notice mild stomach upset. Evening dosing may suit sleep-focused goals; morning or pre-workout timing works for others. Consistency over several weeks matters more than a single high dose.

## Safety, Side Effects, and Who Should Avoid Ashwagandha

Ashwagandha is generally well tolerated in healthy adults at standard extract doses, but it is not risk-free. Reported side effects are usually mild and include **digestive upset, drowsiness, headache, and nausea**. Higher doses or taking it on an empty stomach increase the chance of GI discomfort.

Because ashwagandha may influence **thyroid hormone levels**, people with **hyperthyroidism, hypothyroidism, or those taking thyroid medication** should speak with a clinician before use. It may also have **mild sedative effects**, so use caution if you combine it with sleep aids, anti-anxiety drugs, or alcohol.

**Who should avoid or defer ashwagandha:**

- **Pregnant or breastfeeding women** — Safety data are insufficient; avoidance is the standard advice
- **People scheduled for surgery** — Stop at least two weeks beforehand unless your doctor says otherwise, due to possible effects on blood pressure and sedation
- **Autoimmune conditions** — Theoretical immune-modulating effects mean extra caution for conditions such as lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, or multiple sclerosis
- **Men with hormone-sensitive concerns** — Some data suggest effects on testosterone and, in isolated reports, stress-related reproductive markers; discuss with a doctor if this applies to you
- **Liver disease or elevated liver enzymes** — Rare liver injury reports exist; discontinue and seek care if you develop jaundice, dark urine, or upper abdominal pain

Ashwagandha may interact with **immunosuppressants, sedatives, blood pressure medications, and diabetes drugs**. If you take prescription medication, confirm timing and compatibility with your pharmacist or doctor.

**Practical tip:** Start with one capsule daily for a week, note sleepiness and digestion, then adjust. Stop the supplement and contact a healthcare provider if you develop persistent nausea, rash, or signs of liver trouble.
