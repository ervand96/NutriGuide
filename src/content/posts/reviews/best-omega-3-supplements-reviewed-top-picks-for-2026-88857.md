## What Are Omega-3s and Why Do They Matter?

Omega-3 fatty acids are essential fats your body cannot make on its own. You must get them from food or supplements. The three main types are EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid), DHA (docosahexaenoic acid), and ALA (alpha-linolenic acid).

EPA and DHA come mainly from fatty fish and fish oil. ALA is found in plant sources like flaxseed, chia seeds, and walnuts. Your body converts ALA to EPA and DHA, but the conversion rate is low — often under 10%. For most people, direct EPA and DHA intake is more reliable.

Research links adequate omega-3 intake to several health areas:

- **Heart health:** EPA and DHA may support healthy triglyceride levels and overall cardiovascular function.
- **Brain and eye development:** DHA is a major structural fat in the brain and retina, especially important during pregnancy and early childhood.
- **Inflammation:** Omega-3s play a role in the body’s inflammatory response, though effects vary by dose and individual health status.

Many people fall short. The American Heart Association recommends eating two servings of fatty fish per week. If you rarely eat salmon, sardines, mackerel, or herring, a supplement can help fill the gap — but it is not a substitute for a balanced diet.

**Practical tip:** Aim for a combined EPA plus DHA total that matches your goal. General wellness often falls around 250–500 mg per day from all sources. Higher doses used in research for triglyceride support typically start around 2,000–4,000 mg of EPA plus DHA daily — talk to your doctor before going that high, especially if you take blood thinners.

## How We Chose the Best Omega-3 Supplements

We evaluated omega-3 products with a focus on quality, transparency, and value — not marketing claims. Here is what we looked for:

**EPA and DHA content per serving.** Labels should state exact milligrams of EPA and DHA, not just total fish oil volume. A 1,000 mg softgel might contain only 300 mg of combined EPA and DHA.

**Third-party testing.** Look for certifications from organizations like IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards), NSF, or USP. These verify purity, potency, and low levels of heavy metals and oxidation markers.

**Oxidation levels.** Fresh fish oil has low peroxide and anisidine values. Rancid oil tastes strong or fishy and may be less beneficial. Reputable brands publish freshness data or batch test results.

**Source and sustainability.** Wild-caught small fish (anchovy, sardine, mackerel) are common sources with lower mercury risk than large predatory fish. Some brands note Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) certification.

**Form and tolerability.** Triglyceride-form fish oil may absorb slightly better than ethyl ester form. Enteric-coated capsules reduce fishy burps for sensitive users. Algal oil is a good plant-based DHA and EPA option for vegetarians and vegans.

**Cost per gram of EPA plus DHA.** Total bottle price matters less than how much active omega-3 you get for your money.

We limited recommendations to products available on **iHerb** and **MyProtein**, since those are the retailers NutriGuide links to. We excluded products with vague labeling, missing potency details, or no verifiable quality standards.

**Practical tip:** Store fish oil in a cool, dark place. Refrigerating after opening can slow oxidation. If capsules smell sharply fishy or taste bad, discard them.

## How to Pick the Right Omega-3 Supplement for You

Start with your dietary pattern. If you eat fatty fish two or three times per week, you may not need a high-dose supplement. If you rarely eat fish, are vegan, or want targeted support for heart, brain, or joint health, a daily supplement with clear EPA and DHA labeling makes more sense.

**Check the EPA and DHA numbers.** The active ingredients are eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), not total fish oil volume. A product labeled 1,000 mg fish oil may only contain 300 mg combined EPA and DHA. For general health, many adults aim for roughly 250–500 mg combined EPA and DHA daily from food and supplements. Higher doses are sometimes used for specific conditions, but those should follow medical guidance.

**Choose fish oil or algae based on your needs.** Fish oil remains the most cost-effective way to get higher EPA and DHA doses. **Nordic Naturals Ultimate Omega** and **MyProtein Omega 3 Softgels** are widely available options on iHerb and MyProtein respectively, with labeled EPA and DHA content. Choose algae if you avoid animal products or dislike fish oil, accepting that you may need more softgels or a higher daily cost to match fish-oil potency.

**Look for purity and freshness markers.** Reputable brands test for heavy metals, oxidation, and contaminants. Certifications such as IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards) or clear third-party lab results add confidence, especially for daily long-term use.

**Consider form and tolerance.** Triglyceride-form fish oil is often marketed as better absorbed than ethyl ester form, though both can be effective when taken with a meal containing fat. If you get fishy burps, try taking softgels with food, storing the bottle in the fridge, or switching brands. Enteric-coated capsules may help some people but are not essential for everyone.

**Calculate value per gram of EPA plus DHA.** Bottle size and sale pricing vary on iHerb and MyProtein, so divide the total cost by the milligrams of active omega-3 you actually consume each month. A cheaper bottle with low EPA and DHA per softgel is not always the better deal.

**Practical tip:** Talk to your doctor before starting omega-3 supplements if you take blood thinners, have a bleeding disorder, or are preparing for surgery, since higher doses can affect clotting in some individuals.

The best omega-3 supplement is one you will take consistently, with a dose that matches your diet and goals. Fish oil from **Nordic Naturals Ultimate Omega** or **MyProtein Omega 3 Softgels** covers most people who want reliable EPA and DHA at a reasonable cost, while algae options like **Nordic Naturals Algae Omega** and **MyProtein Vegan Omega 3** fill the same role for plant-based eaters. Read labels for actual EPA and DHA content, buy from retailers you trust, and treat supplements as support for a balanced diet—not a substitute for regular fish, nuts, seeds, and overall healthy eating.
